WHY OVERCOME POVERTY AND HUNGER?

I am not poor! Why should I help in eliminating poverty? Well, we are all humans and are dependent on each other for our needs. Initially, if you see a poor farmer you might get a notion that you are not dependent on him because he seems poor and struggling to have a good living. But wait, where does food on your plate come from? You got your answer, right! Likewise, we are dependent on daily wage laborers, rickshaw pullers, etc. and so are they on us. So eliminating poverty will make the life of the poor ones better which will indirectly reflect in our lives too.

As long as poverty, injustice and gross inequality exist in our world, none of us can truly rest.

Roberta's Story

Waterloo, Iowa

As Roberta pulled up to the holiday food distribution at Northeast Iowa Food Bank the Monday before Thanksgiving, her 9-year-old son Elijah was sitting in the back seat, a computer on his lap, doing schoolwork. Meanwhile, her daughter, the bubbly, rambunctious 4-year-old Eliona, just wanted to charm the volunteers.

“She loves being the center of attention,” Roberta laughed as Eliona popped out of the car, giggling and jumping around. Roberta has a career in home healthcare but needed a little extra help this year affording food for Thanksgiving. She knew the food bank would be there for her.

“This is going to help a lot,” she said as volunteers loaded bags of holiday food – including ham and sides – into her trunk. “We’ll have this for the holiday, and then have leftovers for a while too.” The food helps Roberta make the day special for her family, but she’s focused on more than that.

“It would be a normal day without this food,” she said. “I’m grateful for the food and I’m just really thankful for all the blessings we have.”

Christina 's Story

Wai'anae , Hawaii

As the COVID pandemic reached Hawaii in spring 2020, local farmers were hit hard. With many on very slim margins, the pandemic made operating small family farms incredibly challenging to sustain.

“As business slowed down, we asked ourselves, ‘How are we going to deal with it? Do we still keep planting?’ It all happened so suddenly, but we knew people still needed food,” said Tony Wong Cam of Huang’s Green Leaf Products – a farm in Wai’anae, Hawaii.

But the farm persevered – thanks to a partnership with the Hawaii Foodbank.

The Hawaii Foodbank is a food bank that provides food to families in need.“Thanks to Hawaii Foodbank, we kept planting, and we were able to keep our 35 employees working,” said company matriarch Fanny Gan, Tony’s mother. “For me, I really rely on the food bank, and I believe other farmers are, too.”

In 2019, the food bank purchased 21,000 pounds of pak choy and kai choy from Huang’s Green Leaf Products.The food bank committed to purchasing $750,000 in local agricultural products for its food programs – including more than 21,000 pounds of pak choy and kai choy from Huang’s Green Leaf Products. And in doing so, the food bank helped preserve the family’s farming legacy, which began on a two-acre lot in Wai’anae more than 20 years ago.

“I just needed something to do with the skills my husband had. He’s the one who chose farming,” Fanny Gan says of their humble start. “After working all those years, I’ve learned little by little, and now I enjoy it because I know we’re providing food for a lot of people.”

“When they began, my parents saw a need to provide for me and my siblings. Now, they are providing food and sustenance for many people,” says Tony.

While Tony believes creativity and staying relevant are important, he also knows believing in what you do is what truly brings success. “When COVID-19 hit, our operation of planting remained normal because we believed. We never stopped believing. And when Hawaii Foodbank wanted product, we were ready,” says Tony. “The program really supports local farmers, and the extra demand for locally desired vegetables is something we really appreciate. It has not been easy but hearing how people are receiving our products makes my mom and dad very happy. They feel like they are giving back.”

So far, the Hawaii Foodbank has partnered with 19 local farms, ensuring key sources of agriculture remain sustainable, and fresh, local produce is available to communities facing hunger.

Alma's Story

67-year-old Alma really likes to give and receive hugs. In fact, it might be her favorite thing to do. And that’s because she’s always looking on the bright side of life.

“I have a positive attitude always,” she said. “Life is too short. You’ve got to live life to the fullest.”

That attitude gives Alma strength, even in the face of a challenging time in her life. She’s taking care of her daughter, who faces a disability. And, she manages to work four days a week to help make ends meet.

“I’ve been a workaholic all my life,” she admits proudly. “Always trying to get more hours, do more.”

When Thanksgiving arrived this year, Alma didn’t hesitate to reach out for a little extra help. She visited the Northeast Iowa Food Bank’s holiday food distribution in Waterloo to get ham and other traditional holiday food

“This is a blessing,” she said. “We’d make it; we’d scrape by without this. I’d be sure of it. But this does help.”

Global Hunger Crisis 2023

Hunger Numbers Stubbornly High for Three Consecutive Years as Global Crises Deepen

The 2023 Global Hunger Index shows that since 2015 little progress has been made in reducing hunger. The 2023 GHI score for the world is 18.3, considered moderate. This is less than one point below the world’s 2015 GHI score of 19.1, indicating that progress on reducing hunger has largely stalled. In contrast, between 2000, 2008, and 2015, the world made significant headway against hunger. There has been an increase in the prevalence of undernourishment, one of the indicators used in the calculation of GHI scores, rising from a low of 7.5 percent in 2017 to 9.2 percent in 2022. The number of undernourished people in the world increased from 572 million to 735 million in this period. The 2023 GHI scores are based on data from 2018–2022, including the latest data available in this period for each of the four GHI indicators. Because the vast majority of these data are from 2020 or later, they can more fully capture the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic than the data in previous GHI reports.The increase in the number of undernourished people from 572 million to 735 million during this period further illustrates the deteriorating situation. The 2023 GHI scores, derived from data spanning 2018 to 2022, incorporate the impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic, which has exacerbated food insecurity worldwide. This stagnation calls for renewed efforts and strategies to combat hunger and achieve sustainable food security.

According to the 2023 Global Hunger Index (GHI) scores and provisional designations, nine countries are experiencing alarming levels of hunger, while 34 countries are grappling with serious levels of hunger. Among these, six countries have GHI scores that fall within the alarming range. These countries are the Central African Republic, Madagascar, Yemen, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Lesotho, and Niger.The alarming levels of hunger in these countries highlight the severe challenges they face in ensuring food security for their populations. Factors contributing to these high levels of hunger include political instability, conflict, economic downturns, and climatic shocks, which disrupt food production and access. The situation in these regions underscores the urgent need for targeted interventions and international support to address the root causes of hunger and malnutrition. Sustainable solutions such as improving agricultural productivity, enhancing food distribution systems, and providing emergency food assistance are crucial to alleviating hunger in these critically affected areas. The GHI scores serve as a stark reminder of the ongoing global struggle against hunger and the imperative to intensify efforts towards achieving zero hunger by 2030.

Conclusion: The crises the world has faced in recent years have undermined progress toward ending hunger, and the future portends a continuing onslaught of crises for which the world is not prepared. Underpinning this unfortunate expectation is the acceleration of climate change, which is likely to generate not only direct, weather-related crises such as droughts, flooding, extreme storms, and heat but also indirect crises such as pandemics, conflict, and displacement—all of which can contribute to hunger without adept responses. Given the vast inequality in today’s world, these burdens will be disproportionately shouldered by those who already suffer the most from hunger, poverty, conflict, and poor governance.

Food Donation
Food donations play a crucial role in alleviating hunger and supporting vulnerable communities. Each year, millions of people face food insecurity, unsure of where their next meal will come from. Through the generosity of donors, food banks and charitable organizations can provide nutritious meals to those in need. The act of donating food not only helps meet immediate needs but also fosters a sense of community and compassion, bridging the gap between those who have surplus and those who lack.
A significant portion of donated food comes from individuals, local businesses, and farmers. In fact, a pie chart representing the sources of food donations reveals that donations of dairy products as well as grains make up 25%, while fresh produce contribute 33.3% and rest contribution is done by meat that is 25%. This diverse sourcing ensures a variety of foods are available, from fresh produce to non-perishable items, catering to different dietary needs and preferences. By working together, these contributors create a robust supply chain that supports food banks and shelters across the country.
Food donations not only provide immediate relief but also contribute to long-term sustainability. By preventing food waste, donors help reduce the environmental impact associated with excess food production. Another graph shows the breakdown of how food donations are utilized: 50% goes directly to feeding programs, 30% is stored for emergency reserves, and 20% supports educational and community initiatives. These initiatives include nutrition education and cooking classes, empowering individuals and families to make healthier food choices and reduce their reliance on emergency food aid.
